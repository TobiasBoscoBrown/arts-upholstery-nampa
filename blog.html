Sagging Headliner in Your Car? Here Is What to Do

Art's Upholstery Shop·Nampa, Idaho·6 min read

A drooping headliner is one of the most common interior problems we see, and one of the most satisfying to fix. Here is the plain talk version of what is happening, what actually works, and when to stop fighting it and have it re-covered.

Why your headliner starts to droop

If the fabric on the roof of your car has started to sag, bubble or hang down onto your head, you are not alone, and you did not do anything wrong. A headliner is a stiff board covered in a thin layer of foam-backed fabric. Over time, and especially in a place like the Treasure Valley where a parked car can turn into an oven, the thin foam between the board and the fabric breaks down. Once that foam crumbles, the glue holding the fabric has nothing to grip, and the fabric lets go. It usually starts at the back window or around the edges, then spreads until the whole liner is hanging.

The quick fixes people try first

When a headliner first starts to sag, most people reach for a fast fix, and we understand why. The trouble is that the common tricks rarely last. Twist pins and clear-headed push pins can hold a small section for a while, but they only pin the fabric, not the failed foam underneath, so new sags keep appearing around them. Spray adhesive sounds like the right idea, but spraying glue onto crumbling foam just traps the bad foam in place and leaves a lumpy, uneven roof that often lets go again within a season. Double sided tape, staples and steam all fall into the same bucket. They treat the symptom for a few weeks, then the problem comes back, usually worse and harder to fix cleanly.

How to tell it is time to re-cover it

There is a simple rule of thumb. If you press the fabric back up against the board and it feels powdery or you can feel loose crumbs sliding around behind it, the foam is gone and no glue or pin is going to bring it back. At that point the right move is to have the headliner re-covered, which means taking the board out, stripping off the old material and foam down to the bare board, and bonding fresh foam-backed fabric to it. Done that way, the roof goes back to one smooth, tight piece that will hold for years instead of weeks.

What a professional headliner replacement involves

A proper headliner job is more involved than it looks, which is exactly why a clean result is worth having a shop do it. The headliner board has to come out of the car, and that usually means removing visors, grab handles, dome lights, trim and sometimes the pillar covers. The old fabric and every bit of the broken foam get stripped away so the new material has a clean surface to grab. Then fresh foam-backed cloth or suede is bonded to the board, worked carefully around the curves and openings so there are no wrinkles or air pockets, and trimmed clean. At Art's we also re-cover the A, B and C pillars to match, so when you look up the whole roof reads as one consistent piece instead of a patched repair. One of our recent reviewers had us redo a Honda Civic headliner along with all the pillars in a darker suede, and the turnaround was about a week.

Cloth or suede, and other material choices

When you re-cover a headliner you also get to choose how it looks. A factory style foam-backed cloth keeps things original and is the most common pick. A brushed suede or suede-look fabric gives the cabin a richer, more finished feel and is a popular upgrade, often in a darker shade than the original. Both are foam backed so they install tight and clean. If you are not sure what will look right in your car, bring it by the shop and we will hold a few options up to your interior so you can see them in your own light before you decide.

What it costs, and why we quote in person

The honest answer on price is that it depends on the vehicle. A small two door with simple pillars is a different job from a long SUV with a sunroof, extra handles and three rows of trim. That is why we give you a real number after we look at the car rather than a guess over the phone. What we can tell you is that customers across our reviews describe our pricing as fair and competitive, and that we will tell you straight if a simpler repair will get you where you want to be for less.

Making a new headliner last

Once your headliner is fresh, a little care keeps it that way. Cracking the windows on brutal summer days, using a sunshade and parking in shade when you can all keep the cabin cooler and slow down the heat cycle that ages the adhesive. Avoid hanging heavy items from the grab handles or visors, and wipe the fabric gently rather than scrubbing it. None of this is complicated, and a properly re-covered headliner installed over good foam will outlast the quick fixes by years.
